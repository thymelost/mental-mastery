# Mental Mastery — Onboarding Guide

**Who this is for:** Anyone starting the Mental Mastery framework for the first time.
**What it covers:** What you're doing, why it's structured this way, how to begin, and what to expect.
**Read before:** Anything else.

---

## What Is This, and Why Does It Exist?

The Mental Mastery framework is a systematic training system for developing cognitive self-governance — the ability to accurately perceive what your own mind is doing, monitor it in real time under pressure, and modify it deliberately when you choose to.

The immediate practical motivation: modern information environments are increasingly engineered to influence cognition below the threshold of conscious awareness. Advertising, social media algorithms, persuasion architectures, and emerging neural interface technologies can shape what you notice, what feels true, what you feel pulled toward, and what you value — without you noticing it happening. The standard defenses (critical thinking, media literacy, willpower) operate at a different level than these influences and are inadequate against them.

The goal of this framework is to develop a different kind of defense: not a set of mental rules or beliefs, but a trained perceptual and monitoring capacity that makes covert influence detectable. You cannot push back against something you cannot see. You cannot see it without the prerequisite perceptual infrastructure. Building that infrastructure is what this framework is for.

**This is not:**
- A meditation tradition or spiritual practice
- A therapy protocol
- A productivity or performance system
- Mystical, speculative, or faith-based

**This is:**
- A skills-based training program grounded in neuroscience
- Designed to develop specific, measurable, observable capacities
- Built on evidence about how the brain actually changes with practice
- Honest about what is strongly supported versus what is plausible but not yet certain

## The Core Idea: Three Layers

The framework builds skills in a specific order because later skills are genuinely impossible to do well without earlier ones. This is not a design choice — it reflects the actual dependency structure of cognitive architecture.

```
LAYER 3: Intentional Plasticity
         Deliberately reshape cognitive-emotional patterns you have identified.
         Requires: knowing what you're modifying, and being able to see it while modifying it.

LAYER 2: Metacognitive Monitoring
         Observe your own mental processes in real time — thoughts, emotions, attention, narratives.
         Requires: reliable signal reception from Layer 1.

LAYER 1: Foundational Literacy
         Accurately receive signals from your own body and internal states.
         The prerequisite for everything above it.
```

The central safety principle: **you cannot safely change what you cannot first perceive.** Attempting Layer 3 work (deliberate pattern modification) without solid Layer 2 (real-time monitoring) is not just ineffective — it produces changes you cannot track. This is the primary risk the framework is designed to prevent.

Most people arrive here with some Layer 2 capacity (many people can observe their thoughts to some degree) but unreliable Layer 1 infrastructure (emotional signals are often noise, not signal). The framework builds from the ground up.

## What You Are Actually Training

The full framework contains 41 skills organized across 9 domains. You do not need to know them all now. What matters at the start:

**Layer 1 contains:**
- **Somatic Intelligence (S domain):** Accurately sensing your own body — not meditation, but physical signal detection. Emotional literacy depends on this.
- **Emotional Literacy (E domain):** Naming, differentiating, and tracking emotional states. Without this, emotional signals are undifferentiated noise.
- **Attention (A domain):** Directing, sustaining, and recovering attention deliberately. The basic operational tool for all monitoring work.

**Layer 2 contains:**
- **Cognitive Monitoring (C domain):** Observing your own thought processes as they happen — what kind of thinking is operating, what shortcuts are running, what narratives are constructing themselves.
- **Emotional Self-Monitoring (E2 domain):** Tracking where emotional states come from and how they move through you.
- **Self-Boundary Integrity (B domain):** Distinguishing internally-generated content from externally-introduced content; detecting when you're being moved away from your own values.
- **Meta-Attention (A2 domain):** Monitoring your monitoring — background awareness that runs beneath task engagement.

**Layer 3 contains:**
- **State Modulation (M domain):** Deliberately shifting your own internal states.
- **Pattern Work (P domain):** Identifying, interrupting, and reshaping recurring cognitive-emotional patterns.

The full skill list is in `skill-taxonomy.md`. You don't need to read it before starting.

## Honest Prerequisites

**Time:** The training requires 50–75 minutes per day for the first six phases (roughly 9–18 months of work). This is not optional — the practices are the mechanism by which skills develop. There is no accelerated version.

**Equipment:** A journal and a timer. The app includes both: a built-in voice and text journal and a session timer for every practice. A physical or digital journal outside the app works equally well if you prefer it. You also need the ability to measure your own pulse (a fitness band helps but is not required — a finger on the wrist works).

**Prior experience:** None required. No meditation background, no psychology knowledge, no particular fitness level.

**Psychological stability:** If you have significant unaddressed trauma, chronic dissociation, a history of psychosis, or are currently in mental health crisis, this framework is not appropriate as a standalone intervention. Some practices in Phases 4–5 systematically open access to less-examined aspects of your own processing, which can surface difficult material. See the safety section at the end of this guide.

**Mindset:** The training treats mental skills the same way physical training treats physical skills. Progress is measured by what you can demonstrably do, not what you believe about yourself. You will likely find that your self-assessment of some skills is less accurate than you expect — this is normal, expected, and the framework is specifically designed to detect and correct for it.

## Phase 0: Your First Two Weeks

Phase 0 is not training — it is measurement. Its purpose is to establish where you actually are, not where you assume you are. The outputs determine which track you start on and set baselines that you will compare to later.

**Do these five assessments in order. Spread across 3–5 days — not all at once.**

---

### Assessment 1: TAS-20 (Emotional Awareness Questionnaire)

**What it is:** A 20-item questionnaire measuring how aware you are of your own feelings. Freely available online — search "TAS-20 questionnaire PDF."

**What to do:** Answer honestly. Score yourself (instructions are in the questionnaire). Write your total score and three subscale scores in your journal with today's date.

**What it means:**

| Score | Meaning | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| < 52 | No significant alexithymia | Standard Phase 1 |
| 52–60 | Borderline range | Extended Phase 1 (add 2–4 weeks) |
| > 61 | Clinically significant alexithymia | Somatic-priority track: Phases 1 and 2 extended by 4–8 weeks each |

> **If you score > 61:** This is the most important thing to understand before starting. Alexithymia means your body-to-awareness signal pathway is underdeveloped — you have difficulty identifying and describing your own feelings, and your attention tends toward external events rather than internal states. This is not a flaw or a permanent condition. It is exactly what the framework's first two phases address. What it means for you: move more slowly through Phases 1 and 2 than the baseline schedule. The training will work — it just requires more time at the foundational level.

---

### Assessment 2: Somatic Baseline (Body Scan)

**What to do:** Lie on your back on a firm surface. Slowly move your attention through your body from head to feet. When you notice any physical sensation, name it using a physical word only: *pressure, heat, tension, pulsing, tingling, heaviness, coolness, vibrating, hollow, numb.* Do not interpret ("it feels stressed") — just name the physical quality. If an area is blank (you notice nothing there), note it. Continue for 10 minutes.

**What to record:** How many distinct sensations did you identify? (Count them.) Were any areas consistently blank? Did you find yourself using emotional words instead of physical ones?

**What it means:**

| Sensation count | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0–3 | Very low somatic baseline — extended Phase 1 |
| 4–8 | Low-moderate baseline — standard Phase 1 |
| 9–15 | Moderate baseline — standard |
| 16+ | High baseline |

The most important data here is not the count — it is whether you can distinguish physical sensation from emotional interpretation. If your answers were things like "my chest feels heavy with sadness" rather than "there is pressure in my chest" — that is the key thing to work on in Phase 1.

---

### Assessment 3: Emotional Vocabulary Audit

**What to do:** Set a 10-minute timer. Write down every distinct emotional state you can name — states that feel genuinely different from each other, not synonyms. Work without a reference list. Count the total.

**What it means:**

| Count | Meaning |
|---|---|
| < 10 | Low vocabulary — extended Phase 2 |
| 10–20 | Moderate baseline — standard |
| 20–35 | Above average |
| 35+ | High baseline — watch for overconfidence in Phase 2 |

Also note: are most of your words negative? Positive? A heavily skewed list is itself useful information about your default emotional attention.

---

### Assessment 4: Attention Sustaining Baseline

**What to do:** Sit comfortably. Place your attention on the physical sensation of breathing — specifically the feeling at the nostrils or the rise and fall of the chest. Not the concept of breathing — the physical sensation. When you notice that your attention has drifted to something else (thoughts, plans, memories), make a small mark in your journal and return to the sensation. Do this for exactly 10 minutes.

**What to record:** Number of drift marks. Estimate of your longest continuous stretch without drifting (in minutes).

This is a pure baseline — there is no "good" score here. You are just establishing your starting point.

---

### Assessment 5: Heartbeat Detection Baseline

**What to do:**
1. Sit quietly for 2 minutes.
2. For each interval below: without touching your body or counting your pulse, count the number of heartbeats you can feel internally from START to STOP. Use a timer.
   - Interval 1: 25 seconds
   - (30-second rest)
   - Interval 2: 35 seconds
   - (30-second rest)
   - Interval 3: 45 seconds
   - (30-second rest)
   - Interval 4: 25 seconds
3. Write your count for each interval.
4. Then measure your actual pulse for 60 seconds (index finger on inner wrist). Use that rate to calculate the expected count for each interval.

**Scoring per interval:** Accuracy = 1 − (|your count − expected count| ÷ expected count). Average across 4 intervals.

**What it means:**

| Accuracy | Meaning |
|---|---|
| < 50% | Very low interoceptive baseline — expect extended Phase 1 |
| 50–65% | Low-moderate — standard Phase 1 |
| 65–80% | Moderate — standard |
| 80%+ | High interoceptive baseline |

This measures interoceptive accuracy — how accurately you can sense your own body signals. It is one of the most reliable Track B measures in the framework and will be re-tested throughout Phases 1 and 2 to confirm actual progress.

---

### Phase 0 Output: Your Entry Record

Write a dated entry — in the app's journal or an external one — with:

1. TAS-20 score (total + three subscale scores) → entry track assignment
2. Somatic sensation count → note which areas were blank
3. Emotional vocabulary count (keep the list — you will expand it in Phase 2)
4. Attention baseline: drift count and longest stretch
5. Heartbeat detection accuracy %
6. Your assigned track: Standard / Extended / Somatic-Priority

**This entry will be the baseline you compare everything else to.** Do not skip it, and do not go back and edit it later. The value of a baseline is that it is fixed.

## Choosing Your Entry Track

After Phase 0, you are on one of three tracks:

**Standard Track**
- TAS-20 < 52
- Somatic baseline ≥ 4 sensations
- Emotional vocabulary ≥ 10
- Follow Phase 1 as written — 4–8 weeks

**Extended Track**
- TAS-20 52–60, OR somatic baseline < 4, OR vocabulary < 10
- Phase 1: 6–10 weeks (add 2–4 weeks to each phase where the relevant domain is flagged)
- No practices change — just allow more time for thresholds to be met before advancing

**Somatic-Priority Track**
- TAS-20 > 61
- Phases 1 and 2 are extended by 4–8 weeks each
- Body scan and interoceptive practices take priority over emotional literacy practices for the first weeks
- Emotional literacy work (Phase 2) begins after somatic skills are more established
- All practices are the same; the sequence weights somatic work more heavily early on

**Multiple flags:** If you are flagged for more than one (e.g., low vocabulary AND low somatic baseline), use the more extended timeline. There is no benefit to rushing.

## What Phase 1 Looks Like

Phase 1 begins the actual training. Its focus is narrow and deliberate: body signal reception and basic attentional control.

**Daily time commitment:** ~55 minutes

**The core Phase 1 practices:**

| Practice | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Body Scan (1A) | 30 min, morning | Train sensation detection — S1.1, S1.2 |
| Sensation Labeling (1B) | 10 min, morning | Train physical vs. interpretive language |
| Focused Attention Practice (1C) | 15 min, any time | Train A1.1 (sustaining), A1.2 (directing), A1.3 (recovery) |
| Emotion Check-In (1D) | 2 min × 3/day | Begin E1.1 (primary emotion identification) |
| Heartbeat Detection Check | 10 min, weekly | Track B confirmation that S1.2 is developing |

**What you will notice in Phase 1:**
- The body scan will probably feel blank or boring at first. This is expected. Somatic signal detection requires weeks of practice before it becomes reliable. Stick with it.
- Your attention will drift constantly during the focused attention practice. This is also expected — you are learning to notice the drift, not to eliminate it.
- Emotion check-ins may feel vague or circular ("I feel... fine?"). That uncertainty is the starting point. It improves.

**You leave Phase 1 when** (not before):
- You can identify 5+ distinct physical sensations in a 10-minute body scan
- Your heartbeat detection accuracy has reached ≥ 70%
- You can maintain focused attention for 10+ continuous minutes with fewer than 3 unrecovered drifts
- You can correctly identify your current emotional state in > 80% of structured prompts, and can distinguish 5 of the 7 primary states

All four criteria must be met. Meeting three and rushing into Phase 2 means Phase 2 will stall.

## What to Expect Over the Full Training

The framework spans 9 phases. Here is an honest picture:

| Phase | Approx. Timeline | What You're Developing |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Weeks 1–2 | Baselines established |
| 1 | Weeks 3–10 | Body sensing, basic attention |
| 2 | Weeks 11–20 | Emotional literacy, attentional depth |
| 3 | Weeks 21–28 | Bridge to metacognitive monitoring |
| 4 | Weeks 29–40 | Thought observation, cognitive monitoring, narrative detection |
| 5 | Weeks 41–52 | Self-boundary skills, dual presence, load-condition stability |
| 6 | Weeks 53–56 | Layer 2 verification — gate to Layer 3 |
| 7 | Weeks 57–68 | State modulation, intentional state shifting |
| 8 | Ongoing | Pattern recognition and directed practice |

**Important:** These timelines are estimates, not targets. Progress is gated on threshold criteria, not elapsed time. Some people move faster; some slower. Both are correct if the thresholds are being honestly met.

**What subjective change feels like, and when:**
- **Weeks 1–4:** Practice often feels mechanical. Small perceptual differences begin to appear.
- **Weeks 4–8:** Subjective shifts become more noticeable — emotions feel cleaner, attention feels more controllable.
- **Weeks 4–8 (separately):** Functional connectivity changes in the brain are occurring, but these do not always correspond to the subjective changes. The two are decoupled within the same measurement window. Trust the data from both tracks, not just how you feel.
- **Months 3–6:** The monitoring skills begin to feel less effortful.
- **Months 6–18:** The L2→L3 gate — the most significant boundary in the framework. Do not rush this.
- **Years:** The deepest pattern work and sustained expert-level effects.

**On pace:** Faster is not better. The L2→L3 gate exists because premature Layer 3 work — attempting to deliberately modify patterns before you can accurately perceive them in real time — is the primary serious failure mode in this framework. The gate criteria are not bureaucratic hurdles; they are the minimum evidence that perception is accurate enough for safe self-modification.

## Five Things That Determine Whether This Works

**1. The journal is not optional.**
The journal is the primary mechanism for Track A evidence generation. Skipping entries does not just mean less data — it means some skills (particularly I1.3 Calibration Honesty and B2.4 Identity Stability Monitoring) cannot develop, because these skills depend on longitudinal written record. You cannot hold months of data accurately in working memory. Write the date on every entry. Never delete. The app's built-in journal (text or voice dictation) fulfils this requirement — there is no need to maintain a separate journal if you use it consistently.

**2. The somatic practices are not meditation.**
The body scan in Phase 1 is a physical signal detection practice, not a relaxation or mindfulness exercise. The goal is accurate sensory reporting, not altered states, calm, or insight. Substituting breath-focused meditation is the most common early error and will stall somatic progress.

**3. Both measurement tracks must advance.**
Track A (journals, self-reports, questionnaires) and Track B (physiological/performance measures) must both show progress at each phase gate. Track A alone is not sufficient — the framework is specifically designed to catch the gap between how you think you are doing and how you are actually doing.

**4. Meet the thresholds, then advance. Not before.**
Phase gates have specific criteria because skills in later phases depend on skills from earlier ones in concrete, functional ways. Moving to Phase 2 before meeting Phase 1 thresholds does not save time — it creates problems that have to be unwound later.

**5. Self-assessment will be biased.**
This is expected, not a character flaw. The I1.3 (Calibration Honesty) skill is introduced specifically because practitioners consistently rate their own skills higher than the data supports. The framework's assessment structure is designed to surface this. When your data says you haven't met a threshold, trust the data.

## The Safety Signals

The framework is designed for individuals who are psychologically stable enough to engage with systematic self-examination. In most people, this process is challenging but not harmful. In some circumstances, it can surface material that requires support.

**Pause practice and seek support if any of the following appear and do not resolve within one week:**
- Persistent sleep disruption after beginning a new phase
- Flashback-like experiences during body scan or somatic practice
- Dissociative episodes (feeling detached from your body or surroundings in ways that don't quickly resolve)
- PHQ-9 or DASS-21 scores increasing rather than decreasing or holding stable over multiple weeks of practice
- PHQ-9 score ≥ 15 at any point during the training

None of these is common. But they are real. A good rule: if practice is consistently making life harder rather than temporarily challenging, something needs to be addressed before continuing.

**The L2→L3 gate has additional safety requirements** beyond skill thresholds: PHQ-9 ≤ 9 and no active crisis indicators. Attempting Layer 3 work during a period of significant depressive symptoms would mean attempting deliberate pattern modification at a time when the perceptual accuracy the modification requires is compromised by the depression itself. This is explicitly blocked by the gate criteria.

## Common Starting Questions

**"Do I need to read all the documents before starting?"**
No. Read this guide. Open `training-curriculum.md` to Phase 0 and then Phase 1. That is enough. The other documents are references — you will consult them as specific needs arise.

**"What if I already have a meditation practice?"**
Existing practice is a partial foundation. Familiarity with sustained attention will help in Phase 1. Be careful about two things: (a) the body scan is not breath-focused meditation and requires a different mode of attention — do not substitute your current practice, and (b) existing practitioners tend to have the highest calibration overconfidence (I1.3 problem) because prior practice creates a sense of competence that is real in some respects but does not map precisely to this framework's skill structure.

**"Can I do this faster if I practice more hours per day?"**
Somewhat, but less than you expect. The limiting factor at most stages is not practice hours but consolidation time — the neural changes that underlie skill acquisition happen partly during sleep and rest, not only during practice. More practice per day yields some acceleration, but the timescales are set primarily by biology, not effort. Extended practice beyond about 90 minutes per day in early phases may also increase the risk of practice fatigue and somatic overwhelm.

**"What if I've done therapy or psychological work and think I'm ahead of some phases?"**
Possibly. The Phase 0 assessments will tell you where you actually are. Some people with significant therapeutic backgrounds will move quickly through Phases 1–3. Others will find that therapeutic work, while deeply valuable, trained different skills than this framework targets. The assessments distinguish between them. Let the data guide the decision, not the assumption.

**"Is there any risk in doing this wrong?"**
In Phases 1–5, errors are largely about inefficiency — the wrong practice, or the right practice poorly executed, produces slow progress rather than harm. The risk calculus changes at the L2→L3 gate. Premature Layer 3 entry (modifying patterns before perception is reliable) is the one genuinely serious failure mode. The gate structure prevents this if followed honestly.

**"What does success look like?"**
A practitioner who has completed the framework through Phase 6 can accurately monitor their own internal states in real time under social, cognitive, and emotional pressure; can detect when their state is being driven by external influence rather than internal origin; can identify values conflicts as they occur (not only in retrospect); and has a calibrated, evidence-based picture of their own capabilities. This is the cognitive firewall the framework is designed to build. Phase 8 continues indefinitely — there is no ceiling.

## One More Thing

The framework is built on an uncomfortable premise: that most people, most of the time, have less accurate self-perception than they believe. The research evidence for this is strong. Calibration studies consistently show that people rate their own competencies above what their performance data supports; emotional interpretation studies show that people regularly misattribute the source of their own states; attention research shows that attentional control under load is far weaker than introspection suggests.

This is not a criticism of any individual. It is a description of how human cognition works by default. The framework is built around this reality, not against it. Every gate, every two-track measurement system, every calibration check exists because accurate self-knowledge requires active construction — it does not arrive naturally.

Starting with honest Phase 0 data is how that construction begins.

Neither is required reading before Phase 0, but both are useful context if you find yourself wanting to know *why* a practice is designed the way it is.

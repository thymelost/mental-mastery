## INCONSISTENCIES

**INCONSISTENCY 1 — TAS-20 threshold differs between the progression-model and assessment-tools/curriculum.**

- **Progression-model, Phase 0 "Flags" section:** "TAS-20 > 61 (clinically significant alexithymia)"
- **Training-curriculum, Phase 0, Assessment 1 table:** "> 61 = Clinically significant alexithymia"
- **Assessment-tools, Section A1 table:** "≥ 61 = Clinically significant alexithymia"

The progression-model and curriculum use "> 61" (i.e., 62 or above), while assessment-tools uses "≥ 61" (i.e., 61 or above). This is a borderline-case discrepancy: a person scoring exactly 61 would be assigned different tracks depending on which document they consulted. The standard TAS-20 scoring convention (Bagby et al. 1994) uses "> 61" for the clinically significant cutoff, making the progression-model and curriculum correct, and the assessment-tools wrong by one point.

Separately: the assessment-tools table (A1) states "≤ 51 = Standard" while the training-curriculum table states "< 52 = Non-alexithymic." These are mathematically equivalent (≤51 and <52 both mean "51 or below") so this is not an error, just a notation difference.

This inconsistency in scale definition runs throughout multiple documents and could cause confusion when practitioners try to cross-reference arousal readings from different phases.

## SUBSTANTIVE CONCERNS

**SUBSTANTIVE CONCERN 1 — B2.2 (Incongruence Detection) is essentially untrained by the curriculum.**

This is the most significant training gap in the framework. B2.2 is listed as a prerequisite for B2.3, B2.4, P3.3, and is part of the L2→L3 gate requirement (B2.1–B2.4 functional under pressure). But the only curriculum elements attributed to B2.2 are:
1. Values Document Construction (5A) — a one-time document creation task
2. Reframing Exercise (8C) in Phase 8 — which uses B2.2 as a self-check tool, not as the skill being trained

There is no practice whose primary purpose is developing the detection skill itself — no graduated exercises for noticing in real time when a thought, impulse, or feeling conflicts with established values. The Values Document creates the reference standard, but training the detection of incongruence against that standard is not systematically addressed. By Phase 5, a practitioner is expected to meet a threshold of "Can identify when a thought or impulse is inconsistent with stated core values within 60 seconds of noticing, in > 80% of structured probes" — but no practice in Phase 5 explicitly trains this. The Boundary Challenge Practice (5D) is labeled B2.1 and develops source attribution, not values-alignment detection. The gap between what B2.2 requires and what the curriculum provides is the largest training design gap in the framework.

**SUBSTANTIVE CONCERN 2 — The P3.1–P3.5 progression is underspecified in the curriculum compared to other phases.**

Phase 8 is labeled "Ongoing (no ceiling)" and the Pattern Work skills receive detailed explanations, but the scaffolding for P3.1–P3.5 is notably thinner than earlier phases:

- **P3.1 (Personal Pattern Recognition):** Practice 8A (Pattern Documentation) is well-defined, but predictive accuracy — a key threshold component — is trained only incidentally ("begin predicting when each will appear") without any systematic protocol for developing prediction skill.
- **P3.2 (Pattern Interruption):** The research-grounding and scientific-foundation both note that standard mindfulness does NOT train pattern interruption effectively, and that targeted interruption practice is required. The curriculum's Practice 8B defines a general framework but provides no specific graduated training exercises. The "interruption action" is practitioner-designed, which is appropriate for later phases but means the curriculum provides minimal scaffolding for developing this skill systematically.
- **P3.3–P3.4:** Reframing (8C) and Topology Mapping (8D) are well-specified as ongoing practices but have no progression arc within Phase 8 — no early/mid/late Phase 8 differentiation, no indication of how the practices should evolve as skills develop.
- **The distinction between P3.3 (Deliberate Reframing) and suppression/self-deception** is mentioned in the curriculum (Practice 8C self-check) and in the taxonomy, but there is no structured training approach for developing the capacity to distinguish genuine reframing from motivated reframing. The scientific-foundation notes this as a real risk (P3.3 risk), but the curriculum's only safeguard is the practitioner's self-check question.

**SUBSTANTIVE CONCERN 3 — The I1.1 load-condition skill set tested in B8 differs from what Phase 5 actually trains.**

Assessment-tools B8 (Load-Condition Assessment) tests: A2.1, A2.2, C2.1, C2.6, B2.1, B2.3.
Training-curriculum Practice 5E (Load-Condition Practice) explicitly trains: A2.1, C2.1, B2.3.

Three skills in the B8 assessment — A2.2 (Dual Presence), C2.6 (Narrative Monitoring), and B2.1 (Self-Other Distinction) — are not included in the Phase 5 load-condition training practice. The practitioner is therefore assessed at Phase 5 exit on skills they have not explicitly practiced under load. Dual Presence (A2.2) is trained in Practice 5C but not in load conditions. Narrative monitoring (C2.6) is continued from Phase 4 but not the focus of load-condition work. Self-Other Distinction (B2.1) is trained in Practice 5D under "challenge" conditions but those are not the same as the cognitive/emotional/social load conditions defined in B8.

This creates a structural problem: the gate assessment (B8) tests more than what the curriculum explicitly trains for load conditions. Passing would require spontaneous transfer of skills trained in non-load contexts to load contexts — which is specifically what the research grounding (Beyeler et al., 2017 perceptual learning specificity findings) says does NOT happen automatically. This is the opposite of the framework's stated principle.

**SUBSTANTIVE CONCERN 4 — Arousal scale inconsistency (1–5 vs. 0–10 vs. 1–10) creates calibration problems for the whole framework.**

As noted in Minor Issue 8, the arousal scale used to measure S1.4 is inconsistent across documents. This is more than a documentation issue: the thresholds in the progression-model use both scales. The B2.3 threshold ("S1.4 arousal ≤ 6/10") uses a 0–10 scale, but S1.4 is assessed using a 1–5 scale in Phase 2 (B3). A practitioner who calibrates their arousal detection on a 1–5 scale in Phase 2 and then encounters "arousal ≤ 6/10" thresholds in Phase 5 must mentally convert — but no document explicitly instructs this transition or reconciles the two scales. This affects how practitioners use arousal readings across multiple thresholds (S1.4, M3.1, M3.3, B2.3) and makes the calibration data from Phase 2 non-directly comparable to thresholds stated in later phases.

**SUBSTANTIVE CONCERN 5 — The framework's claim that E2.3 (Affect-Cognition Coupling Detection) can be trained is not strongly grounded in the research base.**

The scientific-foundation provides a solid mechanistic account of affect-cognition coupling (Section 2.4) but the specific claim that real-time detection of this coupling is a trainable skill — as opposed to a retrospective analytical skill — is not supported by any of the seven papers reviewed. The research-grounding document does not include E2.3 in any of its "Confirmed by Convergent Evidence" sections. The taxonomy assigns E2.3 a Phase 4 training slot with a real-time detection threshold ("in ≥ 3 instances per week"), but there is no research basis cited for training the real-time detection specifically. The practice (Decision Review, 4G) is primarily a retrospective journal exercise. The distinction between retrospective analysis (achievable with general cognitive skill) and real-time detection (which requires a different kind of training) is not addressed in the curriculum for this skill.

**SUBSTANTIVE CONCERN 6 — The dependency graph has a potential ordering problem for A2.2.**

The taxonomy dependency table shows A2.2 (Dual Presence) requires: A1.4, A1.5, A2.1. All of A1.4, A1.5, and A2.1 are introduced/completed in Phases 1–3. The progression-model places A2.2 as "(fully functional here)" in Phase 5, which is appropriate. However, A2.2 is listed as a prerequisite for B2.1 (Self-Other Signal Distinction): "B2.1 prerequisites: S1.3, E2.1, C2.1, C2.6."

Wait — B2.1's prerequisites in the taxonomy are: S1.3, E2.1, C2.1, C2.6 (not A2.2). But the progression-model Phase 5 lists A2.2 alongside the B-domain skills as though it is a co-development, not a prerequisite. The scientific-foundation Domain 7 section states: "A2.2 (Dual Presence) is the critical operational skill" for the firewall mechanism and lists it as a prerequisite alongside B2.1 for the Phase 5 function. So A2.2 and B2.1 are trained simultaneously in Phase 5, but A2.2 is functionally required for B2.1 to operate (you need dual presence to monitor for self-other distinction in real time). The Phase 5 design trains them in parallel rather than sequentially, which is workable but the dependency is not made explicit in the phase description.

More importantly: A2.2 appears in the L2→L3 gate as a required component ("A2.2 dual presence stable for ≥ 6 weeks") and as an item in the B8 load-condition assessment — but A2.2 was not included in the Phase 5 load-condition practice (5E) as noted in Substantive Concern 3. The dependency chain works upward but the training chain has a gap.

## RESEARCH GROUNDING ACCURACY CHECKS

Checking 6 specific research claims across the scientific-foundation and taxonomy against what the research-grounding document says those papers found:

**CHECK 1 — Ganesan et al. 2025: SMS-Body subscale finding**

- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 1:** "Focused-attention breath meditation does NOT reliably develop somatic awareness — the SMS-Body subscale showed no group improvement in fMRI neurofeedback RCT (Ganesan et al., 2025)."
- **Research-grounding, Section "Where the Taxonomy Required Revision," R3:** "SMS-Body (somatic awareness subscale) showed no significant group difference in the fMRI NF study; only SMS-Mind improved."

**Accurate.** The research-grounding confirms this finding. The taxonomy's claim is accurately stated.

**CHECK 2 — Yue et al. 2023: FFMQ Observing subscale**

- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 7 (Meta-Attention):** "FFMQ Observing subscale (noticing internal experiences) increased specifically in the mindfulness group, not the active sleep hygiene control (Yue et al., 2023) — the closest self-report marker of meta-attention."
- **Research-grounding, "Meta-Attention / Dual Presence" section:** "FFMQ Observing subscale (noticing internal experiences, thoughts, emotions) increased specifically in the mindfulness group, not the active control group (P6). This is the closest self-report measure to meta-attention."

**Accurate.** The claim is correctly stated and the research-grounding confirms it.

**CHECK 3 — Calderone et al. 2024: Insula thickness and alexithymia**

- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 1:** "Insula cortical thickness increases with concurrent alexithymia decrease after 8-week MBSR (Calderone et al., 2024)."
- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 2:** "somatic-emotion mapping (E1.2) has the strongest neuroanatomical support in this corpus: insula thickness increase + alexithymia decrease after 8-week MBSR (Calderone et al., 2024)."
- **Research-grounding, "Somatic Intelligence Domain" section:** "8-week MBSR increases right insula cortical thickness and somatosensory cortex thickness, with concurrent decrease in alexithymia (P2, n=23). This is the strongest direct neuroanatomical confirmation of somatic-emotion mapping (E1.2)."

**Accurate, with one detail:** The research-grounding specifies "right insula" and "somatosensory cortex" while the taxonomy says only "insula cortical thickness." The taxonomy's version is a simplification (omits "right" and the somatosensory cortex finding), but it is not inaccurate — it is just less specific.

**CHECK 4 — Ganesan et al. 2025: BCT accuracy declined in best-performing group**

- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 3:** "BCT accuracy declined in the group with the strongest neural/subjective improvement (Ganesan et al., 2025)."
- **Research-grounding, "Attention Domain" section:** "BCT accuracy declined in the group showing strongest neural and subjective improvement (P1)."
- **Research-grounding R1:** "BCT accuracy declined in the group with strongest neural and subjective improvement (P1), interpreted as increased decentering awareness reducing mechanical counting accuracy."

**Accurate.** The claim is correctly stated in both documents and the research-grounding confirms the finding with the same interpretation (decentering reduces mechanical accuracy).

**CHECK 5 — Calderone et al. 2024: Hippocampal volume increase**

- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 9 (Pattern Work):** "Hippocampal volume increase (Calderone et al., 2024) and insula/PFC thickness changes support P3.5 (directed neuroplastic practice) with structural evidence."
- **Scientific-foundation, Section 2.1:** "Hippocampal volume increase after 8-week MBSR (Calderone et al., 2024) likely reflects this [hippocampal neurogenesis]."

The research-grounding document does not separately list hippocampal volume in its summary sections, but it confirms the study's character as a systematic review (n=9 studies) of neurobiological changes. The hippocampal volume finding is cited consistently in both the taxonomy and scientific-foundation without contradicting anything in the research-grounding.

However, there is a subtle accuracy concern: the scientific-foundation says hippocampal volume increase "likely reflects" hippocampal neurogenesis, which is speculative. The taxonomy [R] note uses this as support for P3.5 without the same hedging. But this is an internal hedging difference, not a contradiction with the research-grounding.

**CHECK 6 — Beyeler et al. 2017: Gamification and 2-year persistence**

- **Taxonomy [R] note, Domain 9 (Pattern Work):** "Gamification of practice contexts enhances learning durability (effects at 2 years) by recruiting dopamine/acetylcholine systems."
- **Research-grounding, "Directed Neuroplastic Practice" section:** "Gamification-enhanced perceptual learning effects persist up to 2 years (P4, P5) when attentional neuromodulators (dopamine, acetylcholine) are recruited through engaging contexts."
- **Scientific-foundation, Section 2.7:** "Gamification of learning environments enhances plasticity durability (persists 2 years — Beyeler et al., 2017)"

**Potentially inaccurate attribution.** The research-grounding attributes the 2-year finding to both P4 and P5 (Fallon et al. 2009 and Beyeler et al. 2017). However, the scientific-foundation attributes this specifically to "Beyeler et al., 2017" alone. Fallon et al. 2009 is a cochlear implant review; Beyeler et al. 2017 is a visual prosthetics review. The research-grounding appears more accurate in crediting both papers. The 2-year gamification finding likely comes from the broader perceptual learning literature reviewed in these prosthetics papers, not necessarily from a single direct experiment by Beyeler et al. The taxonomy and scientific-foundation's single-source attribution to Beyeler et al. may be an oversimplification that the research-grounding itself does not make.

Additionally, the taxonomy [R] note says gamification recruits "dopamine/acetylcholine systems" as the mechanism, whereas the research-grounding does not specify this mechanistic claim in that section — the mechanism is implied but not stated. The scientific-foundation (Section 2.7) provides the dopaminergic mechanism explanation, which is plausible and consistent with the referenced literature, but the specific linkage "gamification → DA/ACh → 2-year persistence" is a framework inference, not a direct claim from the papers cited.

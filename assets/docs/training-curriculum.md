## How to Use This Curriculum

### What this is
A phase-by-phase practice guide. Each phase contains: the practices to do, how to do them, how long to do them, what to record, and how to know when to move on.

### What this is not
- Therapy. If you have significant trauma, chronic dissociation, a history of psychosis, or are in active mental health crisis, this curriculum is not an appropriate standalone intervention. Some practices in Phases 4–5 can surface difficult material. Work with a clinician alongside this framework if applicable.
- A fixed schedule. Duration ranges are given in weeks, not mandatory timelines. Move on when you meet the threshold criteria, not when the clock runs out. Some people will complete Phase 1 in four weeks. Others will take twelve. Both are normal.
- A meditation tradition. This curriculum draws heavily on contemplative-derived practices but frames them as trainable mechanisms, not spiritual exercises. You do not need prior experience, and you do not need to adopt any particular worldview.

### Time requirements
- **Phases 1–5:** 50–75 minutes per day (split into morning block, brief daytime practices, evening block)
- **Phase 6:** 30–40 minutes per day (assessment-focused)
- **Phases 7–8:** 40–60 minutes per day (some formal practice, increasing integration into ordinary activity)

> **Minimum effective dose (research note):** Four independent studies of mindfulness training under operational military stress conditions (Jha et al., 2015, 2016, 2017, 2020) found that approximately 11 minutes of daily practice is the minimum threshold to maintain the stress-protective capacity of trained skills. Below this threshold, skills trained during low-stress periods erode under high-stress exposure and provide no protective benefit when they are most needed. The schedules above are well above this minimum for good reasons (more practice accelerates development), but if life circumstances reduce available time, do not drop below ~11 minutes of formal daily practice and expect your monitoring skills to hold under pressure.

### Equipment
- Timer (phone works)
- Dedicated journal — physical or digital, doesn't matter, but write longitudinally (date every entry; never delete)
- Pulse measurement (index finger on inner wrist or throat for 30 seconds, multiply by 2) — no device needed, but a fitness band or pulse oximeter makes Track B easier
- Validated questionnaires (administered at phase gates — links/sources noted where relevant; all are freely available online)

### Track A and Track B
Throughout the curriculum, you will see **[Track A]** and **[Track B]** labels:
- **Track A** = subjective/behavioral evidence (journals, self-reports, questionnaires)
- **Track B** = physiological/performance evidence (pulse measurements, duration logs, task accuracy)

Both tracks must show progress to pass a phase gate. Doing well on Track A only means self-report is improving; doing well on Track B only means performance is improving. You need both.

### The journal is not optional
The journal is how Track A evidence is generated and reviewed. Skipping journal entries does not just mean you have less data — it means the B2.4 (Self-Attribution Consistency Monitoring) and I1.3 (Calibration Honesty) skills cannot be developed, because those skills depend on longitudinal written record. You cannot hold months of data accurately in working memory.

## Safety Note

The practices in Phases 4–8 progressively open access to less-examined aspects of your own cognition and emotional processing. In most people, this is benign and clarifying. In some circumstances it can surface material that is temporarily uncomfortable or destabilizing. Signs that you should pause and seek support before continuing:
- Persistent sleep disruption that does not resolve within a week of beginning a new phase
- Flashback-like experiences during body scan or somatic practice
- Dissociative episodes (feeling detached from your own body or surroundings in ways that don't resolve quickly)
- Significant increase in distress (PHQ-9 or DASS-21 scores increasing rather than decreasing over multiple weeks)

These signs are not common, but they are real. A good baseline rule: if practice is consistently making things worse rather than temporarily challenging, pause and reassess.

## Phase 0 — Entry Assessment

**Duration:** 1–2 weeks
**Purpose:** Establish your individual starting point across all Layer 1 domains. Determines your entry track and flags any areas requiring extended early phases.

### What You Do

Complete all five assessments in order. Do not do them all in a single session — spread across 3–5 days.

---

#### Assessment 1: TAS-20 (Toronto Alexithymia Scale)
**What:** A 20-item questionnaire measuring difficulty identifying feelings, difficulty describing feelings, and externally oriented thinking.
**How:** Search "TAS-20 questionnaire PDF" — freely available. Answer honestly. Score yourself (instructions are included with the measure).
**Record:** Total score and three subscale scores in your journal.

| Score Range | Meaning | Track |
|---|---|---|
| < 52 | Non-alexithymic | Standard |
| 52–60 | Borderline | Extended Phase 1 |
| > 61 | Clinically significant alexithymia | Somatic-priority track (extended Phases 1 and 2; see note below) |

> If your score is > 61: the somatic and emotional literacy phases will take longer. The practices are the same, but the threshold dates will be later. Do not attempt to rush through Phases 1 and 2. Alexithymia is not a flaw — it is a biological starting point, and the training is specifically designed to change it.

---

#### Assessment 2: Somatic Baseline (Body Scan)
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**What:** Lie on your back. Starting at the top of your head, slowly move attention through your body. When you notice a sensation anywhere, say or write one word describing it — using only physical terms (pressure, heat, tension, pulsing, tingling, heaviness, etc.). Do not interpret ("it feels sad") or evaluate ("it feels good"). Just name the physical quality.
**Record:**
- How many distinct sensations did you identify? (count)
- Did you find yourself using emotional or interpretive words? (yes/no, note examples)
- Which areas were easy to feel? Which were blank?

| Sensation count | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0–1 | Very low somatic baseline — **somatic-priority track** (anchor protocol; see Phase 1 priority variant below) |
| 2–3 | Low somatic baseline — extended Phase 1 |
| 4–8 | Low-moderate baseline — standard Phase 1 |
| 9–15 | Moderate baseline — standard |
| 16+ | High baseline — may move through Phase 1 faster |

---

#### Assessment 3: Emotional Vocabulary Audit
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**What:** Set a timer. Write down every distinct emotional state you can name — states that feel genuinely different from each other, not just synonyms. Work without a reference list (you can verify afterward).
**Record:** The complete list, and the total count.

| Count | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ≤ 5 | Very low vocabulary — **vocabulary-priority track** |
| 6–9 | Low vocabulary — extended Phase 2 |
| 10–20 | Moderate baseline — standard |
| 20–35 | Above average — may move through Phase 2 faster |
| 35+ | High baseline — you may find Phase 2 relatively easy; watch for I1.3 overconfidence |

After writing your list, count how many are primarily negative, how many positive, how many neutral. Imbalance (e.g., 80% negative) is itself useful data about your emotional landscape.

---

#### Assessment 4: Attention Sustaining Baseline
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**What:** Sit comfortably. Place your attention on the physical sensation of breathing — specifically the feeling at the nostrils, or the rise and fall of the chest. Not the idea of breathing — the physical sensation. When your attention wanders (you notice you've been thinking about something else), make a small mark in your journal and return to the sensation. Do this for exactly 10 minutes.
**Record:**
- Number of drift marks (involuntary departures from the sensation)
- Longest single continuous stretch without drift (estimate in minutes/seconds)
- What pulled your attention most? (work thoughts, random images, planning, physical discomfort)

---

#### Assessment 5: Heartbeat Detection Baseline [Track B]
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**What:** This is the Schandry-type heartbeat detection task adapted for self-administration.
- Sit quietly for 2 minutes first (establish baseline quiet)
- For each interval: without touching your body or counting pulse, count the number of heartbeats you feel internally from START to STOP. Your timer will signal start and stop.
- Run 4 intervals: 25 seconds, 35 seconds, 45 seconds, 25 seconds (in this order, with 30-second rests between)
- After each interval, write your count
- After completing all four, measure your actual pulse for 60 seconds (index finger on inner wrist), and use that rate to calculate the expected count for each interval

**Scoring:**
- For each interval: calculate your accuracy = 1 − (|perceived − actual| / actual)
- Average across 4 intervals

| Accuracy | Meaning |
|---|---|
| < 50% | Very low interoceptive baseline — extended Phase 1 |
| 50–65% | Low-moderate — standard Phase 1 |
| 65–80% | Moderate — standard |
| 80%+ | High interoceptive baseline |

---

#### Phase 0 Output

In your journal, record:
1. TAS-20 score and track assignment (standard / extended / priority for emotional literacy)
   Somatic track assignment (standard / extended / somatic-priority)
   Vocabulary track assignment (standard / extended / vocabulary-priority)
2. Somatic baseline sensation count
3. Emotional vocabulary count (and list)
4. Attention baseline (drift count, longest stretch)
5. Heartbeat detection accuracy %
6. Your assigned entry track based on the above

**Date this entry.** You will return to these numbers at later phase gates to measure actual change.

**Time per day:** ~55 minutes

---

### Practices Introduced in Phase 1

---

#### Practice 1A: Body Scan
**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Skills trained:** S1.1, S1.2
**When:** Morning, before checking phone or email

**Instructions:**
1. Lie on your back on a firm surface (floor or firm bed). Legs uncrossed, arms at sides, palms up.
2. Take three slow breaths. On each exhale, allow your body to settle — don't force relaxation, just stop holding.
3. Begin at the crown of your head. Place your attention there. You are not looking for anything specific — just sensing what is present. Stay for 20–30 seconds.
4. Move slowly downward: forehead, eyes, jaw, throat, shoulders, upper arms, elbows, forearms, wrists, hands (pause on the hands — many sensations are detectable here), fingers. Then move to the chest, upper back, mid-back, lower back, abdomen, hips, pelvis.
5. Continue through the thighs, knees, calves, ankles, feet, toes.
6. When you find a sensation, name it silently using physical terms only:
   - *Good: pulsing, warm, tight, heavy, numb, tingling, cool, pressure, vibrating, hollow*
   - *Avoid: tense (too interpretive — instead: tight), nervous (emotional — instead: fluttery), good/bad, comfortable/uncomfortable*
7. If an area is blank (no sensation detectible), that is data too. Note it. Don't force.
8. If your attention wanders to thought, gently return to the body region where you left off. No judgment.

**What to notice:**
- Which regions are easiest to sense? (usually hands, face, abdomen)
- Which are consistently blank? (often mid-back, thighs in some people)
- Are sensations stable or changing as you observe them?

**What to record:** In your journal after each session — 2–3 sentences: which regions were clearest, which were blank, anything unusual. Note the date and session number.

**Common errors:**
- Trying to relax instead of observe — relaxation may happen but it's not the goal; observation is
- Using emotional or interpretive language for sensations — each time you catch this, rephrase
- Speeding through regions with no sensation — spending equal time on blank regions trains the detection of subtle signals

---

#### Practice 1B: Sensation Labeling
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Skills trained:** S1.1
**When:** Afternoon, seated

**Instructions:**
1. Sit comfortably with eyes closed. Choose one body region (not breath — pick something else: hands, face, abdomen, feet).
2. Place your entire attention on that region. Your only job is to notice sensations and name them, one by one, as they arise. Say the names silently.
3. If a sensation is subtle, lean into it — approach with curiosity rather than effort. Forcing does not help.
4. After 5 minutes, shift to a different body region for the remaining 5 minutes.

**Purpose of this practice vs. body scan:** The body scan moves systematically. This practice develops dwell-time — the ability to sustain attention on a single region long enough for subtle sensations to become perceptible. Many people find that after 60–90 seconds of sustained attention to one region, sensations that were initially absent begin to appear.

---

#### Practice 1A/1B Priority-Track Variant — Anchor Protocol
*(For somatic-priority track only: baseline sensation count 0–1)*

**Rationale:** When interoceptive resolution is very low, the standard body scan produces mostly silence — there is insufficient internal signal for attention to lock onto. This is not failure; it reflects a starting state where the internal channel has low gain. A deliberate, controllable external sensation acts as a spotlight: it gives attention a stable, high-intensity anchor to lock onto first. Once attention is reliably holding the anchor, you can begin expanding bandwidth toward subtler internal signals.

**What to use as an anchor** (choose one per session, rotate across sessions):
- **Temperature:** Hold an ice cube in one hand, or run cold water over wrists for 30 seconds before the session. The resulting temperature sensation in the hand/wrist is the anchor.
- **Pressure:** Press the heel of one hand firmly into the floor. Sustained, steady pressure. The sensation at the contact point is the anchor.
- **Breath at nostrils:** The physical sensation of air passing through the nostrils — the slight coolness on inhale, warmth on exhale. (Not the chest rise, not the idea of breathing — specifically the nostrils.)

**Modified Practice 1A (first 2–4 weeks on priority track):**
1. Begin the session by establishing your chosen anchor. If using temperature or pressure, sustain it actively throughout.
2. Place all your attention on the anchor sensation. Name what you feel using physical terms. Stay on the anchor until you can hold it continuously for 60 seconds with minimal drift.
3. Once the anchor is stable and clear, attempt to extend attention to the nearest body region — the hand if using ice or pressure, the upper lip if using breath.
4. If that extension holds for 30 seconds, try one more region adjacent to it.
5. Stop expanding when sensation becomes absent. Return to the anchor and hold.
6. Record: anchor used, which regions produced sensation, longest continuous hold without drift.

**Modified Practice 1B (first 2–4 weeks on priority track):**
- Use the anchor as your starting object rather than choosing a blank body region.
- After 3 minutes on the anchor, attempt to shift to one adjacent area for the remaining 7 minutes.

**Transition to standard practices:**
Move to standard Practice 1A/1B when you can reliably detect 2+ sensations without anchor assistance across two consecutive sessions. This typically takes 2–4 weeks.

---

#### Practice 1C: Focused Attention (Sensation Object)
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Skills trained:** A1.1, A1.2, A1.3
**When:** Evening

**Instructions:**
1. Sit with back upright. Choose a body sensation as your attention object — ideally breath at the nostrils (the slight coolness on inhale, warmth on exhale), but if that's not perceptible, use the rise/fall of the chest or the sensation of your hands resting on your thighs.
2. Place your attention on that sensation. Stay with it. Your only task is to remain there.
3. When you notice your attention has drifted — you've been thinking — note the content briefly ("planning," "memory," "worry") and return to the sensation. The return is the training, not the staying.
4. Do not count drifts during the session — this interrupts recovery. Record the approximate total after.

**The distinction this practice trains (A1.2):** At some point you will notice the difference between:
- *Choosing* to place attention on the sensation (volitional)
- *Being pulled* away from it (involuntary)

This distinction — chosen vs. pulled — is the attentional discrimination you are developing. You cannot see it immediately; it emerges over sessions. When you do notice it, write about it in your journal.

**Record after each session:**
- Session duration
- Approximate number of drifts (rough estimate — 5, 10, 20+)
- Longest continuous stretch without drift (in minutes)
- Was attention directed or was it pulled back each time?

---

#### Practice 1D: Primary Emotion Check-In
**Duration:** 2 minutes × 3 daily
**Skills trained:** E1.1 (begins)
**When:** Morning, midday, evening — set alarms

**Instructions:**
1. When the alarm sounds, stop what you are doing. Sit or stand still.
2. Ask: *What am I feeling right now?* Name one primary emotional state from this list: fear, anger, sadness, joy, disgust, surprise, contempt. If you feel nothing discernible, note "unclear."
3. Do not analyze — just name. This is a 2-minute scan, not a therapy session.
4. Write the result in your journal with the time: *"2:15pm — anger (mild)"*

**Important:** If you consistently get "unclear" or find yourself always writing the same state, that is information, not failure. The goal in Phase 1 is establishing the habit and building baseline data.

### Phase 1 Exit Assessment

Before moving to Phase 2, complete all of the following:

**Track A:**
- [ ] Body scan verbal report: identify and locate 5+ distinct physical sensations using only physical terms, consistently across 3 sessions in the same week
- [ ] Attention sustaining: 10+ continuous minutes with < 3 self-reported unrecovered drifts, consistent across 5 consecutive sessions
- [ ] Attention direction discrimination: can correctly identify > 75% of whether attention was chosen or pulled, in a 20-minute session with post-session reflection
- [ ] Attention recovery: drift-to-return time is decreasing trend in your log (not a fixed number — a trend)
- [ ] E1.1: correctly identifies primary emotional state in > 80% of check-ins across a 1-week review (cross-reference entries against what you know about that day)

**Track B:**
- [ ] Heartbeat detection accuracy ≥ 70% averaged across 3 consecutive weekly checks
- [ ] Attention sustaining duration consistently logged (≥ 5 sessions showing duration and drift count)

**If all boxes are checked:** Move to Phase 2.
**If some are not:** Identify the specific gaps and extend Phase 1 targeting those practices. Do not extend everything — only what is below threshold.

## Phase 2 — Emotional Literacy & Attentional Depth

**Duration:** 4–8 weeks (6–12 weeks on extended track)
**Primary skills:** S1.3, S1.4, E1.2, E1.3, E1.4, A1.4, A1.5
**Time per day:** ~65 minutes

---

### Practices Introduced in Phase 2

---

#### Practice 2A: Physiological Journal
**Duration:** 5 minutes
**Skills trained:** S1.3
**When:** Morning, immediately after waking, before getting up

**Instructions:**
1. Do not check your phone first. Lie still for 60 seconds.
2. Open your journal and record across these four body systems:
   - **Heart:** Is it fast, slow, moderate? Steady or irregular? Note a number if you can estimate.
   - **Breath:** Deep or shallow? Slow or fast? Any restriction in the chest or diaphragm?
   - **Gut:** Any tension, heaviness, emptiness, fluttering, nausea, neutrality?
   - **Muscle baseline:** Any areas of obvious tension right now, before you've started moving?
3. Note the overall arousal impression: 1 = very calm/sluggish, 3 = neutral/rested, 5 = alert/activated, 7 = tense/agitated, 10 = overwhelmed

**Purpose:** You are building your personal neutral reference. After 2 weeks of consistent morning records, patterns will emerge — your typical morning baseline. Deviations from that baseline become detectable. This is S1.3.

---

#### Practice 2B: Emotion-Body Map Construction
**Duration:** 25 minutes × 2 sessions per week (not daily)
**Skills trained:** E1.2
**When:** Choose 2 days per week with intentional time

**Instructions:**
Each session, work on a single emotion:
1. Choose one emotion from your vocabulary list (work through them across sessions). Start with emotions you experience relatively frequently.
2. Take 5 minutes to recall a specific memory when you felt this emotion moderately strongly — vivid enough to bring back some of the feeling, not so intense that it overwhelms. This is induction, not immersion.
3. Once you have a mild version of the feeling present, turn your attention to your body. Run a quick body scan specifically looking for changes from your neutral baseline. Where is this emotion in your body? What does it feel like physically?
4. Draw or write a body map entry for this emotion:
   - Name of emotion
   - Body regions where you felt it
   - Physical description of each region (use sensation language, not emotional language)
   - Intensity (0–10)
   - How quickly did it come? How quickly did it fade?
5. At the end of the session, let the emotion settle. Do 2–3 slow breaths and briefly scan back to neutral.

**What you are building:** A personal dictionary that links each emotional state to its specific somatic signature in *your* body. Over weeks, this map becomes a detection system — when you notice a somatic signature, you can read the corresponding emotional state without having to work it out cognitively.

---

#### Practice 2C: Emotional Vocabulary Expansion
**Duration:** 10 minutes × 3 sessions per week
**Skills trained:** E1.3
**When:** Any time — pairs well with evening

**Instructions:**
**Session type 1 — Distinction work (do this first 2 weeks):**
Choose a pair of similar emotions (e.g., anger and frustration; sadness and grief; anxiety and dread; embarrassment and shame). Spend 10 minutes writing the distinction between them: What is functionally different about how they operate? What triggers each? What does each lead to? How do they feel differently in the body? The goal is precision, not definition — you are carving out conceptual space.

Good pairs to work through: anger/frustration/irritation, sadness/grief/melancholy, anxiety/dread/apprehension/worry, shame/guilt/embarrassment, joy/delight/contentment, fear/alarm/panic, contempt/disgust/disdain, tenderness/affection/love, envy/jealousy, curiosity/interest/fascination.

**Session type 2 — Naming specificity (weeks 3+):**
At the end of each day, review your emotion check-ins and ask: can I be more specific? Instead of "anxious," can I say "low-level dread about a specific outcome"? Instead of "irritated," was it "frustrated with my own competence" or "irritated by unpredictability"? Rewrite the check-in entries with greater granularity.

---

#### Practice 2D: Real-Time Gradient Log
**Duration:** Active throughout day (< 1 min per entry)
**Skills trained:** E1.4
**When:** Triggered — log immediately when you notice any emotional shift

**Instructions:**
This practice runs throughout the day, not in a scheduled session. Set up a system for quick capture — a small notebook, a notes app, a voice memo.

When you notice any emotional shift — any change from your morning baseline — capture it immediately:
- Emotion name (as specific as you can)
- Intensity (0–10)
- Time
- What preceded it (what were you doing/thinking)

The key is **early detection.** A Level 2 entry is more valuable than a Level 7 entry, because Level 7 you would have caught anyway. You are training the detection of subtle precursors — the slight chest tightening that precedes full anxiety, the small jaw tension that precedes anger.

**Target:** By end of Phase 2, most of your entries should be at 1–4 intensity. If your entries are consistently 5+, you are only catching things that are already loud.

---

#### Practice 2E: Distraction Resistance
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Skills trained:** A1.4
**When:** Afternoon — in a non-ideal environment

**Instructions:**
Same as Practice 1C (Focused Attention on Sensation Object), but done deliberately in the presence of distraction:
- Week 1–2: mild background noise (music, street noise)
- Week 3–4: more active distraction (a public space, a room with other people)
- Week 5+: while experiencing mild emotional activation (after a challenging interaction, when slightly stressed)

The goal is not to be unaffected by distraction — it is to notice when the pull of distraction occurs and consciously choose whether to follow it or return to the sensation.

**Record after each session:**
- Environment type (quiet, mild noise, active space)
- Number of distraction pulls (different from drifts — a pull you recognize but resist counts differently than one you follow without noticing)
- What types of stimuli pulled most?

---

#### Practice 2F: Aperture Shifting
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Skills trained:** A1.5
**When:** Evening, after Focused Attention session

**Instructions:**
This practice works with the width of your attentional field.
1. Begin with **point focus** (2 minutes): Place attention on a single, small sensation — the tip of your nose, one fingertip, the spot between your eyebrows. Make the attentional object as small as possible. Nothing outside it.
2. **Gradual expansion** (3 minutes): Slowly widen your attention outward — from the single point to your whole hand, then your whole arm, then your entire body, then include the space immediately around you. Move slowly, sensing at each level.
3. **Full field** (2 minutes): Awareness is open — no single object is primary. Everything is equally present. Body sensations, sounds, temperature, light. You are not looking at anything; you are receptive to everything.
4. **Gradual contraction** (3 minutes): Slowly narrow back from full field to body, to one limb, to a single point.

**Record:** Can you hold the full field without a single element dominating and pulling focus? Can you hold point focus without the surrounding field bleeding in? These are the markers of developing aperture control.

### Weekly Track B Check — Arousal Crosscheck
**Frequency:** Once per week
**Method:**
1. Before checking your pulse, estimate your current arousal level: 0–10 scale (0 = completely calm/flat, 10 = maximally activated/tense)
2. Measure your actual resting HR (60 seconds, index finger on inner wrist)
3. Record the pair: (subjective estimate, actual HR)
4. After 4 weeks you will have a personal calibration curve — what HR corresponds to which subjective level for you. This is your S1.4 calibration data.

### Phase 2 Exit Assessment

**Track A:**
- [ ] Physiological journal: has entries across ≥ 14 consecutive mornings. Can identify "my typical morning neutral" with 4 body system readings
- [ ] Emotion-body map: documents ≥ 5 distinct emotions, each with body location and physical sensation description
- [ ] Can identify current emotional state from somatic cues alone (without cognitive interpretation) in > 70% of trials: test by noticing body signature first, naming emotion, then checking — match rate > 70%
- [ ] Emotional vocabulary audit (repeat from Phase 0): can now name and distinguish ≥ 20 distinct states
- [ ] Gradient log: ≥ 60% of entries are at intensity 1–4 (not just catching loud signals)
- [ ] Aperture shifting: can transition between point focus and full field awareness in < 30 seconds; can hold each for 60 seconds

**Track B:**
- [ ] Heartbeat detection accuracy ≥ 70% (maintained from Phase 1 or reached now)
- [ ] Arousal crosscheck: personal calibration curve established (4+ weeks of paired data); subjective estimate within 2 points of expected for ≥ 70% of weekly checks

**Safety instruments (administer now if not already done):**
- [ ] PHQ-9: complete and record — this is your baseline score for emotional regulation tracking. If ≥ 15, consult a clinician before continuing
- [ ] DASS-21: complete and record all three subscale scores — baseline for stress, anxiety, and depression tracking

## Phase 3 — Layer 1 to Layer 2 Bridge

**Duration:** 4–8 weeks
**Primary skills:** A2.1, C2.1, C2.2, I1.3 (introduced)
**Time per day:** ~60 minutes

This phase introduces the first Layer 2 skills while Layer 1 continues to consolidate. The shift in orientation is important: you are no longer just sensing — you are now beginning to *observe the observer.*

---

### Practices Introduced in Phase 3

---

#### Practice 3A: Meta-Attention Probes
**Duration:** 5 seconds per probe, 8–12 times daily
**Skills trained:** A2.1
**When:** Random throughout day — set irregular alarms or use natural triggers (doorways, phone unlocks, beverage)

**Instructions:**
When the probe moment arrives, pause for 5 seconds and answer internally:
*"Where is my attention right now?"*

Not: what am I thinking about (content). But: where is the spotlight of attention pointing?

Possibilities: external task, another person, a problem, a memory, a future scenario, a body sensation, the environment, or genuinely unclear.

Write the answer in your running log (1–3 words: "planning tomorrow," "physical pain," "conversation replay").

**The distinction you are developing:** There is a difference between *being* in a thought and *noticing* that you are in a thought. The first is automatic. The second is the beginning of A2.1. Most people, when first asked "where is your attention," notice their answer is a half-second delayed — they have to quickly backtrack to notice what they were doing. Over weeks, the delay shortens. Eventually you will sometimes notice in real time, without needing the probe.

---

#### Practice 3B: Thought Observation Sit
**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Skills trained:** C2.1
**When:** Morning (replace body scan 3 days per week; keep body scan on other 4 days)

**Instructions:**
1. Sit comfortably, upright. Eyes closed.
2. Your only task is to watch thoughts arise and pass. You are not trying to stop thoughts, modify them, analyze them, or judge them. You are watching them the way you watch clouds move.
3. When a thought appears, notice it and — silently, internally — label it: *"I am having the thought that..."* and complete the sentence with whatever it is.
4. Then let it go. Not forced letting go — just not grabbing it. If a thought leaves naturally, fine. If it stays, continue labeling: "I am still having the thought that..."
5. The moment you notice you have been *inside* a thought rather than *watching* it — lost in the content, merged with it — gently return to watching.

**The core distinction:** "I am late for the meeting" (identification — you are the thought) vs. "I notice I am having the thought that I am late for the meeting" (observation — you are watching the thought). This distinction does not feel significant in description. In practice, with repetition, it begins to produce a genuine perceptual shift.

**Record after each session:**
- How often were you watching vs. merged?
- What types of thoughts were hardest to watch without getting pulled in? (emotionally charged? Plans? Criticisms of self?)
- Any moments of clear observation? Describe briefly.

---

#### Practice 3C: Cognitive Mode Journal
**Duration:** 1 minute × 5 times daily
**Skills trained:** C2.2
**When:** Set 5 irregular alarms spaced through the day

**Instructions:**
When the alarm sounds, write: *"I was just in [mode]."*

Modes to recognize:
- **Analyzing** — deliberate problem-solving, working through logic
- **Planning** — arranging future events or scenarios
- **Ruminating** — replaying past events, usually with emotional charge
- **Daydreaming** — wandering, associative, no particular goal
- **Narratizing** — constructing or rehearsing a story (often about self)
- **Evaluating** — judging: self, others, situations
- **Observing** — present-moment noticing with low narrative
- **Working** — absorbed in task (minimal narrative)
- **Reacting** — responding to an emotional trigger in the moment
- **Unclear** — genuinely unsure

Over weeks, you will notice your habitual distribution. Most people have 2–3 dominant modes that account for the majority of their mental time. This distribution is useful data.

---

#### I1.3 Introduction: End-of-Phase Calibration Check
**Duration:** 30 minutes (once, at Phase 3 end)
**Skills trained:** I1.3
**When:** Before moving to Phase 4

**Instructions:**
1. For each of the Phase 1 and Phase 2 skills, rate your current ability on a 1–5 scale (1 = barely begun, 3 = functional but inconsistent, 5 = reliable and solid). Write these ratings in your journal.
2. Now go back and look at your actual Phase 1 and Phase 2 exit assessment records. Compare your ratings to what your data shows.
3. Where your self-rating is more than 1 point higher than your data supports, flag these as **overestimations**.
4. Where your self-rating is more than 1 point lower than your data supports, flag these as **underestimations**.

**What to do with this:**
- Consistent overestimation (most ratings above data): this is a calibration bias that must be corrected before Phase 4. Spend an additional 1–2 weeks specifically tracking evidence for the skills you overrated.
- Consistent underestimation: less dangerous, but also worth addressing — you may be working harder than necessary on skills you have already developed.
- Random variation: normal — a mix of over and under without systematic direction.

**Record the output of this check in your journal with the date.** This is the beginning of I1.3 as a skill, not just an assessment.

## Phase 4 — Metacognitive Core

**Duration:** 8–12 weeks
**Primary skills:** C2.3, C2.4, C2.5, C2.6, E2.1, E2.2, E2.3
**Time per day:** ~65 minutes

This is the densest cognitive phase. The practices here require active thinking about thinking, and they will feel awkward at first. That awkwardness is the friction of developing a new skill — push through it, but don't force speed.

---

### Practices Introduced in Phase 4

---

#### Practice 4A: Process Awareness Journal
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Skills trained:** C2.3
**When:** Evening

**Instructions:**
This journal is not about what you thought today. It is about *how* you thought.

Review a period of cognitive activity from today — a decision, a conversation, a piece of work, a planning session. Ask:
- What mode was I in? (Use C2.2 vocabulary)
- What shortcuts did I take? What did I assume without checking?
- Was I reasoning forward (from evidence to conclusion) or backward (from preferred conclusion to supporting evidence)?
- What would I have had to think more carefully to actually know, rather than assume?

Write 3–5 sentences. Specific is more valuable than general. "I assumed the project was going well because I hadn't heard complaints" is more useful than "I made some assumptions."

**Over weeks you will notice patterns:** recurring shortcuts, particular types of reasoning collapse, specific contexts where your processing quality drops. These are C2.3 data and they will become P3.1 material later.

---

#### Practice 4B: Cognitive Load Checks
**Duration:** 1 minute × 3 daily
**Skills trained:** C2.4
**When:** Before a demanding task, midpoint, and at end of workday

**Instructions:**
Before beginning: rate your current cognitive capacity on a 1–5 scale.
- 1 = severely depleted (post-major stress, very tired, ill)
- 2 = low (tired, distracted, post-emotionally taxing event)
- 3 = baseline (normal functional state)
- 4 = alert (rested, focused, good conditions)
- 5 = peak (highly rested, motivated, optimal conditions)

Then: carry out the task. Afterward, note whether your actual performance felt consistent with your pre-task capacity estimate.

**Why this matters:** The PFC operates on a metabolic budget. Stress, fatigue, emotional load, and decision overload all deplete it. A depleted processor running at 60% capacity but believing it is at 100% is producing subtly unreliable outputs. C2.4 is catching that gap before it produces consequential errors.

---

#### Practice 4C: Assumption Log
**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Skills trained:** C2.5
**When:** Evening, after Process Awareness Journal

**Instructions:**
Review your day. Find 1–3 moments where you:
- Treated an inference as an observation ("I *saw* that she was annoyed" — did you observe annoyance directly, or infer it from behavior?)
- Skipped a step in reasoning without noticing ("Obviously X leads to Y" — does it? What's the actual mechanism?)
- Filled in missing information with a default assumption ("He didn't respond, so he must be ignoring me")

For each: write what the assumption was, what you actually had evidence for, and what you would need to actually *know* rather than infer.

**The practice becomes self-reinforcing:** Once you start catching assumptions in retrospect, you begin catching them in real time during events. That real-time catch is C2.5 functioning at threshold.

---

#### Practice 4D: Narrative Catch Practice
**Duration:** Active throughout day (instantaneous when triggered)
**Skills trained:** C2.6
**When:** Throughout day — no scheduled session, entirely triggered

**Instructions:**
A narrative is a self-coherent sequence of meaning: "I did X, which proves Y about me, which means Z will happen." Narratives feel like reality. They are constructed.

Throughout the day, practice noticing when one starts. The signal is a distinctive feeling of *coherence* or *inevitability* about a sequence of thoughts — as if a story has taken over. When you notice it:

Say internally: **"I am telling myself a story about [X]."**

Do not stop the story, analyze it, or decide if it's true. Just name it as a story. That is the entire practice.

**Record in your journal** each narrative catch: the theme and the time of day. Patterns will emerge (certain contexts generate more narrative than others; certain emotional states are always narrative-accompanied).

---

#### Practice 4E: Emotion Source Log
**Duration:** 2 minutes per entry, triggered
**Skills trained:** E2.1
**When:** Triggered — after any significant emotional state (> 4/10)

**Instructions:**
When you notice a significant emotional state (using E1.4 gradient detection), add a source attribution:
- *Internal* — arising from a memory, belief, anticipation, or ongoing thought pattern
- *External* — arising primarily from an environmental trigger, social interaction, or media exposure
- *Mixed* — clear elements of both

Then, briefly: what specifically seems to be the source?

Example entry: *"3:30pm — anxiety (6/10) — source: internal — specifically: anticipating a difficult conversation tomorrow."*

**The goal:** Over weeks, you will see that some emotional states have very clear origins (internal vs external) and some feel genuinely ambiguous. That ambiguity is important data — it is precisely where influence operates most easily.

---

#### Practice 4F: Contagion Audit
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Skills trained:** E2.2
**When:** Once per week, Sunday evening (or equivalent)

**Instructions:**
Review the past week. Identify 1–3 moments when your emotional state shifted in a way that, on reflection, seems to have originated from another person, a media environment, or a social situation — rather than from your own internal processing.

For each instance:
- What was the source? (specific person, content type, environment)
- What emotion was transferred and at what intensity?
- Were you aware of the shift as it was happening, or only retrospectively?
- What, if anything, would have helped you notice earlier?

**Over weeks:** You will develop a map of your personal contagion vulnerabilities — certain people, certain content types, certain social environments that reliably shift your state. This is E2.2 developing into predictive capacity.

---

#### Practice 4G: Decision Review
**Duration:** 10 minutes × 3 per week
**Skills trained:** E2.3
**When:** Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday evenings (or equivalent spread)

**Instructions:**
Choose one decision or conclusion you reached this week. Review it specifically for affect-cognition coupling:
- What was your emotional state when you reached this conclusion?
- How might that emotional state have shaped the conclusion? What would you have concluded in a different emotional state?
- Can you identify the coupling pattern by name? (catastrophizing, motivated reasoning, availability bias under fear, positivity bias under joy, anger-activated approach bias, sadness-activated loss focus)

The goal is not to invalidate your conclusions — many of them may still hold. The goal is to see the emotional weather through which you reasoned, rather than treating your conclusions as if they were reached in a vacuum.

## Phase 5 — Self-Boundary & Dual Presence

**Duration:** 8–12 weeks
**Primary skills:** B2.1, B2.2, B2.3, B2.4, A2.2, I1.1 (begins), I1.2 (begins)
**Time per day:** ~65 minutes

This is the core protective layer. The skills here are the ones that answer "is this thought mine?" — and they require everything built in the previous phases to function reliably.

---

### Practices Introduced in Phase 5

---

#### Practice 5A: Values Document Construction
**Duration:** 40–60 minutes (one-time construction; regular updates)
**Skills trained:** B2.2, B2.3

**Instructions:**
This is not an aspiration document ("I want to be honest"). It is a behavioral description of who you actually are, what you actually care about, and how you actually operate when functioning well.

Write in 4 sections:
1. **Core values** (3–7): Each as a single sentence. Not "integrity" but "When I tell someone I will do something, I keep my word even when it is inconvenient." Specific, behavioral.
2. **Non-negotiables** (3–5): Things you will not do regardless of circumstance. Again specific. "I do not deceive people to avoid conflict" not "I am honest."
3. **Characteristic responses**: How do you typically respond to the following — criticism of your work? Requests that ask too much of you? Situations where you have more information than others? Loss? Write 2–3 sentences each.
4. **What operating against your values feels like**: Somatically. What does your body do when you are about to violate a core value? (Many people report a specific physical signal — a constriction, a hollow feeling, a reluctance in the chest.) If you don't know yet, that's fine — come back and add this as you develop E1.2 and E2.1 further.

**Date the document and save it.** Update it deliberately, never passively drift it.

---

#### Practice 5B: Self-Description Baseline
**Duration:** 30–45 minutes (one-time; weekly reviews thereafter)
**Skills trained:** B2.4

**Instructions:**
Write a description of who you currently are — not who you want to be, not your history. Your current self. Cover:
- How you currently think (habitual modes identified in C2.2/C2.3 work)
- What you currently care about
- How you currently relate to others
- What currently characterizes your internal landscape (anxiety-prone? Quiet? Cynical? Curious? Defended?)
- What you notice about yourself through the Layer 1–3 work so far

This document becomes the baseline against which drift is measured. **It should feel slightly uncomfortable** — a genuinely honest self-description always does. If it doesn't, you're writing an idealized version.

**Date and sign it.** Once per week thereafter, open it, read it, and spend 5 minutes answering: *Am I still this person, or has something shifted?* Document your observations. Drift is only visible longitudinally.

---

#### Practice 5C: Dual Presence Training
**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Skills trained:** A2.2
**When:** Daily — during an ordinary activity

**Instructions:**
Choose a daily activity: reading, cooking, exercise, a conversation, commuting.

While engaged in the activity normally, maintain a background layer of awareness of your internal state. You are not pausing the activity to check in — you are running both simultaneously.

The internal monitoring tracks:
- Current emotional tone (gradient log level)
- Body arousal level (S1.4)
- Attentional quality (are you present or drifted?)

**Start with low-demand activities** (week 1–2: walking, eating). Gradually increase the cognitive demand of the foreground activity (week 3–4: reading; week 5+: working, conversations).

**The training test:** When the activity ends, you should be able to give a running account of how your internal state changed during the activity without having had to stop the activity to check. If you cannot, the monitoring was not continuous — it was periodic.

**Track B assessment (Phase 5 gate):** You will be tested on a dual task: perform a reading comprehension task while being randomly probed about internal state. Task performance should not drop below 85% of your rested baseline.

---

#### Practice 5D: Boundary Challenge Practice
**Duration:** 30 minutes × 1 per week
**Skills trained:** B2.1
**When:** Weekly — structured session

**Instructions:**
These are graduated scenarios designed to provide material for B2.1 (self-other signal distinction) and B2.3 (value anchoring under pressure). Work through these categories across weeks:

**Week 1–2: Media exposure scenarios**
Watch or read a piece of content specifically designed to elicit a strong emotional or opinion response (political content, advertising, emotionally charged editorial). As you consume it:
- Track emotional state changes in real time (Gradient Log)
- Note when the content seems to be shaping what feels "true" or "right"
- After: attribute each emotional shift — internal or contagion? Did your views on anything shift? Does that shift feel like yours?

**Week 3–4: Social pressure scenarios**
Recall or enter a situation where someone is exerting social pressure (wanting you to agree, behave a certain way, validate their position). Consciously:
- Notice when your stated position begins to drift from your actual position
- Notice the somatic signal that accompanies the pressure
- Practice returning to values alignment and noticing how that feels physically different from compliance

**Week 5–6: Sustained perspective exposure**
Spend 15 minutes consuming a coherent perspective you find somewhat uncomfortable. After, check:
- Do you still hold your previous position? If it changed, can you articulate why using your own reasoning vs. "it just feels different now"?
- Is the new feeling of convincingness coming from evidence or from repeated exposure?

**Record after each session:** What shifted? What held? What somatic signals were present?

---

#### Practice 5E: Load-Condition Practice
**Duration:** 20 minutes × 3 per week
**Skills trained:** I1.1 (begins), A2.1, A2.2, C2.1, C2.6, B2.1, B2.3
**When:** Times when you are already moderately loaded

**Instructions:**
This practice is done in conditions of genuine (not manufactured) mild-to-moderate load:
- After a cognitively demanding work session
- During mild emotional activation (following a difficult interaction)
- Under mild time pressure
- **After mild physiological arousal** (brief vigorous exercise — 5 minutes brisk walking/stairs, or cold water face immersion for 30 seconds): this fourth condition is required at least once per month, and is mandatory before the L2→L3 gate. Cognitive and emotional load alone are insufficient — noradrenergic stress specifically degrades PFC-dependent monitoring via a mechanism that bypasses effort and motivation (Raio et al., 2013). Skills must be tested at the level where they actually need to work.

During the loaded condition, run all six checks in sequence:
1. **5 min — Meta-attention (A2.1):** Can you notice where your attention is right now? Probe 3 times at random moments in this window. Record accuracy.
2. **3 min — Thought observation (C2.1):** Can you watch thoughts arise without merging with their content? Label 3 thoughts as events ("I had the thought..."). Record whether you caught identification or not.
3. **2 min — Narrative awareness (C2.6):** Is there a running story operating right now? Name it if present (e.g., "replaying the meeting," "worrying about X"). Simply naming counts.
4. **2 min — Source distinction (B2.1):** Is your current emotional or cognitive state internally generated, or being driven by the external load context? Can you locate the signal's origin? Record your judgment.
5. **2 min — Dual presence check (A2.2):** Can you maintain background awareness of your internal state while still engaged with the task context? Rate quality 0–10.
6. **2 min — Values check (B2.3):** Can you locate your values alignment right now? Does your current orientation match your stated values? Record yes/no and any tension noticed.
7. **4 min — Log:** Record quality ratios for all six skills (load quality ÷ rested quality, estimated). This is your I1.1 baseline data.

**Record:** All six quality ratios plus load type. Over weeks you will see which skills degrade earliest under which load types.

---

#### Practice 5F: Recovery Time Log
**Duration:** Ongoing measurement after disruptive events
**Skills trained:** I1.2 (measurement)
**When:** After any significant disruption (argument, stress event, bad news, intense emotional experience)

**Instructions:**
Immediately after a disruptive event, note the time and your current monitoring quality (0–10 scale: 0 = monitoring completely offline, 5 = partially functional, 10 = fully functional).

Check again at: 5 min, 10 min, 15 min, 30 min, 60 min. Record quality at each point.

Recovery = the point where you reach your pre-disruption monitoring quality for the first time. Note the clock time.

**What you're building:** A personal recovery curve. Over weeks of data, you will see your typical recovery time and whether it is improving. This is I1.2's Track B evidence.

---

#### Practice 5G: Incongruence Detection Drill
**Duration:** 5 minutes daily
**Skills trained:** B2.2 (Incongruence Detection)
**When:** Evening — after the Values Document (5A) is complete

**Prerequisites:** Values Document must be written and contain ≥ 5 specific behavioral values before this practice begins.

**Instructions:**
Each evening, review three notable thoughts, impulses, or felt pulls from the day. For each one:
1. State it explicitly: *"I was pulled toward...," "I thought...," "I felt the impulse to..."*
2. Hold your Values Document in mind. Ask: is this thought/impulse/feeling aligned with a stated value, neutral, or in tension with one?
3. If in tension: name the specific value being contradicted and describe the tension in one sentence. Do not resolve it — just detect and name it.
4. Rate your confidence in the detection: certain, probable, or uncertain.

**What you're training:** The real-time retrieval and comparison process — not document creation, but the live detection of incongruence against an established reference standard. The Values Document provides the reference; this practice develops the detection habit.

**Record:** Three detection attempts each session; flag rate (what proportion showed tension); confidence level. Over weeks, your detection speed should increase and your uncertainty rate decrease.

**Common error to watch:** Confusing values tension with mere preference violation. Values tension involves a genuine conflict with a deeply held value, not just doing something you slightly prefer not to do.

---

#### Practice 5H: Recovery Speed Training
**Duration:** 10 minutes × 3 per week
**Skills trained:** I1.2 (active training — not just measurement)
**When:** After a naturally occurring disruption, or using the deliberate induction below

**Instructions:**
This practice complements the Recovery Time Log (5F) by actively working the recovery process rather than only measuring it.

**After a natural disruption:**
1. Note your current state — rate monitoring quality 0–10 and note what is offline (attention? somatic tracking? value awareness?).
2. Apply deliberate parasympathetic activation: 4-second inhale → 7-second hold → 8-second exhale, × 3 cycles. Do not rush.
3. Check state again. What has returned? Rate 0–10.
4. Deliberately re-engage one monitoring skill at a time: first somatic tracking, then attention, then thought observation. Notice the sequence.
5. Record time to baseline and which sequence worked.

**Deliberate induction option (for practice weeks with no natural disruptions):**
Vividly recall a mildly frustrating or activating event from the past 48 hours. Spend 2 minutes replaying it with full sensory detail until you notice a genuine shift in your internal state. Then immediately apply steps 2–5 above.

> **Source monitoring caution:** The vividness of a recalled memory creates a mild source monitoring hazard — a vivid reconstruction can feel more emotionally current than it actually is, and the emotional response it generates may not accurately represent how you felt in the original event. This practice is specifically for generating physiological activation to train recovery skills. Do not use the emotional response from deliberate induction to draw conclusions about the original event, the people involved, or your current feelings about it. The activation is real; the interpretive content that accompanies it is reconstructed and unreliable.

**What you're training:** The active recovery sequence (parasympathetic reactivation → PFC re-engagement → monitoring baseline recalibration) as a deliberate skill, not just an emergent process. Over weeks, this sequence should become faster and more reliable.

### Orientation: Practice Quality Monitoring (I1.4 — Prerequisite Awareness)

Read this before the gate. I1.4 is formally introduced in Phase 7, but understanding its logic now matters.

I1.4 is about ensuring the quality conditions for genuine neural plasticity are active in every Layer 3 session. The research basis (cochlear implant and visual prosthetics literature) shows that behavioral relevance is a necessary gate for lasting change: the brain does not consolidate practice that feels trivial or runs on automatic. Four conditions are required for each Layer 3 session to produce real change:

1. **Behavioral relevance** — the target is connected to something that matters in your actual life, not just a drill
2. **Appropriate challenge** — neither too easy (no plasticity signal) nor overwhelming (stress response dominates learning)
3. **Contextual variety** — practicing in different real-world contexts prevents skills from overfitting to controlled conditions
4. **Intentional engagement** — deliberate awareness is active during the session, not habitual execution

In Phase 6, begin noticing whether your current practices meet these criteria. You do not need to score them yet — just observe. Notice when a session feels rote versus alive. In Phase 7, you will formally rate every session against these four criteria using Practice 7E (Practice Quality Assessment). Developing the observational habit now means Phase 7's formal I1.4 practice will not feel unfamiliar.

**Time per day:** ~55 minutes

You are now in Layer 3. The practices here do not just observe — they deliberately modify. I1.4 is introduced here and is mandatory from the first session.

### Practices Introduced in Phase 7

---

#### Practice 7A: Voluntary Relaxation Protocol
**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Skills trained:** M3.1
**When:** Daily, varied context (do not always practice in the same place)

**Instructions:**
Choose one relaxation method. Work with the same method for at least 2 weeks before switching:

**Method A — Progressive somatic release:**
Systematically move attention through body regions (as in Body Scan), but at each region, invite the muscles to release tension — not force, invite. Stay 30 seconds per region. The difference from the body scan: you are now making a deliberate change, not just observing.

**Method B — Breath regulation:**
Slow exhale-extended breathing: inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 6–8 counts. The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system directly. Repeat for 10 minutes, then allow breathing to normalize while maintaining body observation for 10 more minutes.

**Method C — Physiological sigh:**
Double inhale (quick inhale through nose, then second inhale to fully top off lungs), followed by slow complete exhale through the mouth. Repeat 3 times. Then sit quietly for 15 minutes with normal breathing and body observation.

**Track B measurement:** Before beginning: rate arousal (1–10) and measure HR. After completing: rate arousal and measure HR again. Threshold = subjective reduction of ≥ 2 points and HR reduction of ≥ 5 bpm, consistently across ≥ 8 sessions.

---

#### Practice 7B: State Shifting Protocol
**Duration:** 20 minutes
**Skills trained:** M3.2
**When:** Daily

**Instructions:**
First: define your 3 target states clearly. Use the cognitive mode vocabulary and somatic signatures you have developed. Good starter states:
- **Alert-focused**: high attentional engagement, low narrative, mild arousal, ECN-dominant
- **Open-curious**: broad aperture, low judgment, medium arousal, receptive
- **Calm-stable**: low arousal, low narrative, grounded body awareness, wide-slow breath

For each state, write a description: the somatic signature, the cognitive mode, the breath quality, a memory or context where you naturally inhabited this state.

**The practice:**
1. Spend 5 minutes in your current state as-is — just observe it clearly. Name what state you are in.
2. Choose a target state. Shift to it using any available means (breath, attention, body posture, recalled memory).
3. Hold for 5 minutes. During this time, continuously verify you are actually in the target state (not just thinking about it).
4. Shift to a second target state. Hold for 5 minutes.
5. Return to your natural baseline state.

**Record:** Shift time (how long to enter target state), hold quality (did it drift?), somatic markers confirming you were in the target state vs. just thinking about it.

---

#### Practice 7C: Pre-Task Arousal Calibration
**Duration:** 3 minutes (before demanding tasks)
**Skills trained:** M3.3
**When:** Before significant cognitive or emotional demands — meetings, creative work, difficult conversations, high-stakes decisions

**Instructions:**
1. Before the task: check current arousal (S1.4). Rate 0–10.
2. Ask: what arousal level would optimize this task? (High-stakes detail work → moderate/low; creative exploration → moderate; physical performance → moderate/high; complex emotional conversation → moderate)
3. If you are over: use a brief version of voluntary relaxation (3 deep slow exhales, body scan, 60 seconds)
4. If you are under: brief activation (faster breathing, mild physical movement, recall a motivating context)
5. Proceed.

**Track B:** Log pre-task estimate, target, and post-task assessment of whether you were actually calibrated correctly. Error rate should decrease over weeks.

---

#### Practice 7D: Emotion Modification Practice
**Duration:** 20 minutes × 3 per week
**Skills trained:** M3.4
**When:** After a naturally occurring moderate emotional state, or using mild induction

**Instructions:**
This practice requires an emotional state to work with. Use a recent memory (mild induction — intensity 3–5/10) or work with a currently present state if one is available.

1. Apply E1.4 and E2.1 first: identify the emotion, its intensity, its somatic signature, its source.
2. Now: apply a modification. Options:
   - **Reappraisal**: reinterpret the meaning of the situation. What is a genuine alternative meaning that the facts equally support?
   - **Perspective shift**: view the situation from outside yourself. What would a neutral observer see?
   - **Somatic de-escalation**: using M3.1 breathing, invite the body to reduce the physiological component of the emotion.
3. After the modification: check the somatic signature. Is it reduced? Is it still present (it should be — fully absent means suppression, not modification)?
4. Rate intensity before and after.

**Key distinction to maintain:** You are modifying intensity and duration, not eliminating the signal. An emotion modified from 7 to 3 is a successful outcome. An emotion that drops to 0 with the somatic signature gone should be examined — that pattern suggests suppression.

---

#### Practice 7E: Practice Quality Assessment (I1.4)
**Duration:** 5 minutes — applied to every Phase 7 and Phase 8 session
**Skills trained:** I1.4
**When:** At the end of every formal practice session in Phases 7 and 8

**Instructions:**
After each session, rate it against 4 quality criteria (each 1–5):
1. **Behavioral relevance**: Did this practice feel genuinely meaningful — connected to something that matters to you? (1 = going through motions, 5 = fully invested)
2. **Appropriate challenge**: Were you working at a level where you were engaged but not overwhelmed? (1 = too easy/automatic, 5 = too hard/flooded; target is 3–4)
3. **Contextual variety**: Were you practicing in a varied context today vs. repeating the same setting? (1 = always same chair/time/setting, 5 = genuinely new context)
4. **Intentional engagement**: Were you actively present for the practice or running on autopilot? (1 = mechanical execution, 5 = fully engaged)

**Record the four scores and the total.** If total < 12, identify which criteria are dragging it down and make a deliberate adjustment next session. The adjustment itself is the I1.4 skill.

### Stress-Inoculation Requirement (Phase 7, Weeks 5–12)

From the midpoint of Phase 7 onward, at least 25% of your M3.1 and M3.4 practice sessions should be conducted in conditions of mild physiological arousal — not just cognitive or emotional load, but genuine physiological activation. Options:

- Complete a brief bout of vigorous exercise immediately before a Voluntary Relaxation session (7A)
- Practice Emotion Modification (7D) while your body is still in a slightly elevated physiological state (post-exercise, post-cold exposure, post-brief stress event)
- Deliberately enter a situation that generates mild physical arousal (public speaking, competitive context, physical challenge), then apply M3 skills in that context

**Why this matters:** State modulation skills trained exclusively in calm, quiet conditions remain context-specific. Raio et al. (2013) demonstrated that PFC-dependent regulation fails under acute noradrenergic stress via a mechanism that is independent of effort and motivation. Skills that work in a quiet chair may not work when your system is physiologically activated. The only way to know is to test them there — and the only way to make them reliable there is to practice in those conditions. Layer 3 is for skills that work in life, not only in practice. Record these stress-inoculation sessions separately in your practice log.

### Practices Introduced in Phase 8

---

#### Practice 8A: Pattern Documentation
**Duration:** 10 minutes, daily
**Skills trained:** P3.1
**When:** Evening

**Instructions:**
This journal entry has a specific format. You are building a **Pattern Library** — a catalogue of your recurring cognitive-emotional patterns.

For each new pattern you notice, create an entry:
- **Pattern name** (give it a short name you can recognize: "the competence doubt spiral," "the pre-conflict withdrawal")
- **Trigger**: what reliably precedes the pattern
- **Sequence**: what happens in order (emotion → thought → behavior tendency)
- **Somatic signature**: what it feels like in the body
- **Context**: where and when does it appear most?
- **First observed**: date

Over weeks, your Pattern Library will grow. Review it monthly — old patterns may shift, new entries may be refinements of existing ones.

**Predictive accuracy practice:** Once you have ≥ 3 patterns documented, begin predicting when each will appear. Before entering a known trigger context, note in your journal: "I predict Pattern X will appear." Check afterward. This builds the predictive accuracy component of P3.1.

---

#### Practice 8B: Pattern Interruption Protocol
**Duration:** Active practice (no fixed session)
**Skills trained:** P3.2
**When:** Triggered — when a Pattern Library pattern begins

**Instructions:**
For each pattern in your library, designate:
1. **The earliest detectable point**: what is the earliest signal that the pattern has started? (A somatic shift? A particular thought type? A behavioral tendency?)
2. **The interruption action**: a specific, pre-decided action you will take when you notice the early signal. It should be:
   - Brief (5–15 seconds)
   - Physically distinct from the pattern behavior
   - Feasible in the context where the pattern appears
   - Not suppressive (you are not trying to kill the pattern's content — you are refusing to let it run automatically to completion)

**Examples of interruption actions:**
- Pause, take one deliberate breath, name the pattern out loud to yourself: "That's the competence doubt spiral."
- Change physical position (stand if sitting, shift posture)
- Direct attention deliberately to a body sensation for 10 seconds before continuing
- Write the pattern name in your journal and the trigger context

**Record after each attempt:** Pattern name, trigger, whether interruption was attempted, whether it succeeded (pattern did not complete) or failed (pattern completed despite attempt). Track success rate over time.

---

#### Practice 8C: Reframing Exercise
**Duration:** 20 minutes × 2 per week
**Skills trained:** P3.3
**When:** Two fixed evenings per week

**Instructions:**
Choose one belief, narrative, or meaning structure you hold — preferably one that appears in your Pattern Library or that has been flagged in your Process Journal.

1. State it clearly: "I believe / I tell myself / The story I hold is: [X]."
2. Generate 3 alternative framings that are:
   - Equally consistent with the available evidence
   - Not simply the opposite ("optimistic reframe" is not the goal — genuine alternatives are)
   - Genuinely different in their implications
3. For each alternative: write what would be true about your situation, your choices, and the future *if* this framing were accurate instead.
4. Then return to your original framing. Does it still feel as inevitable? Has your relationship to it changed?

**The crucial self-check:** Ask after every reframing session — "Am I doing genuine reframing or motivated reframing?" Genuine reframing involves holding uncertainty about which frame is more accurate. Motivated reframing selects the most comfortable frame. The B2.2 (Incongruence Detection) skill you developed in Phase 5 is what catches the difference.

---

#### Practice 8D: Topology Map Sessions
**Duration:** 40 minutes × 1 per week
**Skills trained:** P3.4
**When:** One fixed weekly session

**Instructions:**
The topology map is a living document — a structured model of your cognitive-emotional architecture. It is built from Pattern Library entries, Reframing work, Process Journal patterns, and Assumption Log patterns.

**Structure:**
Draw or write a network showing:
- **Nodes**: significant beliefs, emotional patterns, habitual responses, core values, recurring concerns
- **Connections**: how nodes reinforce or trigger each other ("When I feel [X], it triggers [Y]"; "This belief makes [Z] feel inevitable")
- **High-traffic clusters**: areas of your network that have many connections — these are likely key leverage points for change

**Each week:**
- Review the existing map
- Add anything new from the week's Pattern Library entries or journal
- Note any connections between existing nodes you hadn't previously seen
- Identify 1–3 nodes that you think are most influencing current behavior or experience

**The topology map is a working hypothesis, not a truth.** It is your best current model of your own cognitive architecture, built from introspective observation. Its accuracy depends on the quality of the monitoring skills underneath it. It is updated, not completed.

---

#### Practice 8E: Directed Practice Cycle
**Duration:** 60 minutes × 1 (design session); then daily 20-minute practice sessions for ≥ 6 weeks
**Skills trained:** P3.5
**When:** Design session at beginning of cycle; daily practice thereafter

**Instructions:**
Each directed practice cycle targets a single, specific pattern change — one node or connection in your topology map that you have decided to deliberately reshape.

**Design session (60 min):**
1. Select a target: identify a specific pattern from your topology map that you want to change. Be specific: not "I want to worry less" but "I want to interrupt the anticipatory threat-scanning pattern that runs every morning."
2. Define the desired outcome: what would replace the current pattern? Be concrete.
3. Design the practice: what specific cognitive, emotional, or behavioral repetition would build the replacement pattern? The practice must:
   - Be directly relevant to the target (behavioral relevance gate)
   - Be at an appropriate challenge level (not too easy, not overwhelming)
   - Be varied across contexts (to prevent context-specificity)
   - Be integrated with I1.4 quality monitoring
4. Set the duration: minimum 6 weeks of daily 20-minute sessions.
5. Define the pre/post measures: how will you know if the pattern has changed? (Frequency counts in Pattern Library? Predictive accuracy shifts? Somatic signature intensity changes?)

**Execute the cycle for ≥ 6 weeks.** After 6 weeks, assess the pre/post measures. Document the results. Then either continue the cycle (if change is partial) or begin a new cycle on a different target.
